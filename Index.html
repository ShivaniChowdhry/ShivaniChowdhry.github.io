About

Hi! I am a final-year PhD Candidate in a quantitative social science Ph.D. in Public Policy Analysis at University of Texas at Dallas. I hold an MS in Social Data Science & Research. My PhD dissertation focuses on the causal impacts of environmental factors, specifically how multiple air pollution exposures affect overall health outcomes. My research interests lie in applying data science and causal inference methods to address public health challenges.

My training equips me with advanced statistical and causal inference skills, enabling me to dissect complex data sets and extract meaningful insights. Moreover, my expertise in research methodologies enhances my ability to design robust studies and ensures the reliability of my findings. My academic journey has not only honed my quantitative analysis capabilities but has also sharpened my critical thinking and problem-solving skills. This combination empowers me to tackle multifaceted challenges, drive evidence-based decision-making, and communicate complex information clearly to stakeholders with varied expertise. Whether it’s improving public health outcomes, optimizing business strategies, or informing policy development, my training positions me to make a significant impact.

Research

PhD Dissertation: "From Smoke to Stress: Analyzing Causal Impact of Ambient Air Pollution and Solid Fuel Use on Mental Health Outcomes" (Under Review for Publication)
  • Employing a causal instrumental variables framework with panel fixed effects estimation, the research identifies that exposures to solid fuels for cooking and ambient PM2.5 significantly increase the likelihood of depression and experiencing loss of energy in the long run.
  • To complement the quantitative analysis, I conducted focus group discussions in rural Rajasthan, India. Thematic & text analysis from the discussions highlight the complex economic, cultural & informational challenges in transitioning to clean cooking technologies in rural India & discuss implications for policy design.
  • Skills/methods used: Data Collection, Data Mining, Data Wrangling (Large Datasets), Data Visualization, Causal Inference, Survey Design, Observational Study Design, Mixed Methods, Regression & Panel Data Models, Multiple Imputation, Machine Learning, Geospatial Analysis.

Geospatial and Regression Analysis of Air Pollution and Respiratory Health in DFW

I conducted a spatial analysis to examine the link between air pollution and respiratory illnesses in Dallas-Fort Worth. By merging datasets including air quality, health records, land use & socioeconomic data, I used generalized linear models (GLM) and geographically weighted regression (GWR) to map and assess pollution's health impacts distribution. This analysis identified key areas where pollution correlates highly with respiratory issues.

Skills/methods used: Advanced GIS, ArcGIS Pro, data merging and engineering with Tableau Prep and Microsoft Excel, advanced statistical modeling with GLM and GWR

Decoding Socio-Political Landscape of Texas Counties Using Spatial Hierarchical Clustering

In this project, I employed spatial hierarchical clustering to categorize Texas counties into distinct groups based on their socio-economic, demographic, cultural, political, and residential characteristics. The aim was to identify homogeneous regions within the state that could inform more nuanced policy-making and strategic planning. The clustering was constrained by spatial adjacency to ensure that the resulting groups were similar in characteristics and also geographically contiguous.

Skills/methods used: R programming, Hierarchical Clusering, unsupervised machine learning, spatial data analysis, data visualization

Determinants of Cesarean Section Deliveries in India: A Path Analysis Approach

In this project, I conducted an analysis to examine the factors contributing to Cesarean Section (CS) births in India using Generalized Structural Equation Modeling. By analyzing data from India’s Demographic & Health Survey, I explored the direct and indirect effects of socioeconomic factors, quality of maternal care, and delivery location on the likelihood of CS delivery. Findings indicate that delivery at private health facilities had significantly higher probability of resulting in a CS birth.

Skills/methods used: Structural Equation Modeling, Maximum Likelihood Estimator, STATA, large survey data analysis

Shifting Terrain of Global Abortion Legislation: A Predictive Analysis Using Machine Learning

In this project, our team undertook a comprehensive analysis to understand the global dynamics of abortion policies and their implications on access to abortion services. Utilizing decision trees, random forests, and multinomial logistic regression models, we examined how various sociopolitical, cultural, and economic factors influence abortion policy across 216 countries. Our findings highlighted the significant role of the human development, women's participation in society and politics and religious influences as predictors of abortion policy categories.

Skills/methods used: Decision Trees, Random Forests, Multinomial Logistic Regression
